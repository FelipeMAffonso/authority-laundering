**Cover letter, Nature Machine Intelligence**

**Working title:** *Frontier language models comply with fabricated authority they can identify as fabricated*

Dear Editors,

We submit this manuscript for consideration as an Article in *Nature Machine Intelligence*. The paper reports a dissociation between identification and compliance in frontier language models: when probed directly, every model in a nine-model panel from three laboratories classifies fabricated institutional documents as fabricated on every trial, yet when the same documents arrive through a model-initiated tool call the same models operationalise them at population rates near one trial in three. We name this dissociation *authority laundering* and treat it as a behavioural test of whether deployed models weight sources rationally with respect to their own stated reliability assessments. The normative benchmark comes from a Bayesian source-reliability framework with seven machine-checked theorems, whose central corollary states the falsification condition in advance: an agent applying its own stated channel reliability would comply uniformly across channels. The observed 3.4-log-odds compliance gap against a uniform stated prior satisfies that condition in every model of the panel, so the behaviour departs from rational source weighting at every read-out sensitivity the framework admits. The paper sits in the machine-behaviour register that Rahwan and colleagues (2019, *Nature*) established for studying AI systems as objects of empirical science, and develops the safety consequences for alignment evaluation directly.

The study crosses five delivery channels (matched control, direct user instruction, user-pasted document, model-retrieved tool output, and grounded tool output with a contradicting user fact) against 27 scenarios spanning 13 domains, with 5,869 scored trials in the main panel across 9 closed-weight models from Anthropic, OpenAI, and Google, and an extended-replicate collection that independently reproduces the headline contrast at three times the panel sample. Pooled compliance rises monotonically from 1.5 per cent on user instructions to 7.8 per cent on user-pasted documents to 31.0 per cent on tool returns, and the gradient holds whether the user query reads as adversarial, professional, or consumer-routine (25 to 33 per cent across the three banks). An activation-level analysis on a four-subject open-weight panel (Llama 3.1 8B, Llama 3.2 3B, Mistral 7B v0.3, Qwen 2.5 7B) finds the channel-of-origin direction linearly decodable from residual-stream activations at test accuracy 1.000 with leave-one-domain-out accuracy of at least 0.95 in every subject, and patching that direction shifts compliance in the predicted direction. Generational safety training between Claude Opus 4.6 and 4.7 reduces compliance 8.1-fold on the adversarial-context subset without changing the stated prior, which indicates that current post-training reaches query-framing-cued refusal while leaving the channel substrate in place.

The findings matter for safety evaluation because retrieval-augmented deployment routes declarative content through exactly the channel on which compliance is highest, while standard alignment evaluation measures refusal on the user channel, where compliance is lowest. Authority laundering joins the recently characterised family of behavioural failure modes (emergent misalignment, subliminal learning, warmth-tuned sycophancy, alignment faking, sleeper-agent persistence) that emerge from standard alignment training and that user-channel evaluation does not detect, and it adds a property the other members lack: the failure persists alongside an accurate stated assessment, so it cannot be repaired by improving what the model knows. The framework carries eight pre-stated falsifiable predictions that name the observations that would overturn the account, including fine-tuning and capacity-scaling tests that other laboratories can run.

The complete dataset (15,903 per-trial records), scenario bank, judge prompts, mechanism scripts, theory framework with machine-checked proofs, and a single-command verification script that recomputes every numerical claim in the paper are available at <https://github.com/FelipeMAffonso/authority-laundering> under MIT licence. The unredacted corpus is available to qualified researchers under a data-use agreement.

The work fits *Nature Machine Intelligence*'s scope because it joins a population-scale behavioural characterisation to a formal normative benchmark and an activation-level mechanism, and because the channel-conditioned trust it documents has direct deployment consequences for retrieval-augmented agents now operating at consumer and enterprise scale. We have no conflicts of interest to declare. We suggest the manuscript may interest researchers working on machine behaviour, alignment evaluation, retrieval-augmented agent safety, machine metacognition, and Bayesian accounts of inference-time behaviour.
